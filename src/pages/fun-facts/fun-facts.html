Fun Facts

Nicaragua: Fun Facts

  • The country has the lowest crime rate of any Central American country and is considered to be one of the safest countries in which to travel.
  • The Nicaraguan economy is based mostly on agriculture, tourism, mining and manufacturing.
  • You can get 30 Nicaraguan Cordobas (their national currency), for $1.
  • Nicaragua has a population of around six million people and 1.3 million of them live in its capital city of Managua.
  • Nicaraguans don’t name their streets. Your address is given by whatever major landmarks you happen to live near (such as three blocks north of the Wawa).
  • The official language is Spanish but the native dialects and English Creole are also spoken.
  • Nicaragua celebrates religious freedom by adopting  no national religion; it is a secular state. However, 85% of the population is Roman Catholic.
  • Baseball is the national sport and many professional baseball leagues exist. Soccer (football) comes second.
  • The Nicaraguan culture has been influenced from Spanish, British, African and Caribbean roots.
  • Though it is the largest country in Central America, it has the lowest density of population.
  • The Nicaraguan people are fairly young: 36% of the population is under 14 years of age. Median age is 21.
  • Nicaraguans are strong and resilient people who place great value on family and family life. They have shown a great deal of perseverance through times of great adversity and have tremendous national pride.
  • The national dish of Nicaragua is “Gallo pinto”: a combination of white rice and small cooked red beans, often eaten for breakfast but can be found on any meal.
  • Corn is the Nicaraguan’s staple food. Nacatamales are corn flour dumplings filled with veggies and cooked wrapped inside plantain leaves. Corn is also used to make many different traditional drinks.
  • Nicaragua is the birthplace of Ruben Dario, a poet who was extremely influential in the 1900s Latin America poetry community and is world renowned.
  • Nicaragua is famous for folk dances, especially its “Palo de Mayo”. Music combines guitar and wooden marimba or flutes and drums. Dancing is a part of all holidays.
  • Traditional arts include ceramics and earthenware still made in pre-Colombian designs, silver working and gold filigree, wood carving and embroidery.

Geography and Climate

  • Central America’s largest lake is Lake Nicaragua,  which provides the water supply for much of Nicaragua. The lake contains the largest lake island in the world with 430 volcanic islands in the lake.
  • Nicaragua’s famous island, Ometepe (in Lake Nicaragua), is comprised of a Dual Volcano (Concepcion and Maderas Volcanos). It is the only volcano in the world that is fed by two separate flows of magma.
  • Lake Nicaragua is home to the only fresh water sharks in the world, the bull sharks. They swim up Rio San Juan from the Atlantic Ocean and once in the lake, adapt themselves to live in fresh water.
  • Nicaragua produces no oil, but has fierce winds, tropical sun and rumbling volcanoes making it a renewable energy paradise. By 2020, renewable energy will generate 90% of the country’s electricity.
  • Nicaragua has 70 areas of protected regions of water and land to protect their endangered species. These include species of monkeys, boa constrictors, jaguars, sloths, green turtles and sea turtles.
  • Nicaragua’s wettest months are from June to October and its driest season from December through May. The eastern, or “Mosquito coast” of Nicaragua receives  the most rainfall in the country.
  • Managua lies on a geological fault line that seismologists predict will cause the city to experience a severe earthquake approximately every 50 years. A 1972 earthquake destroyed 90 percent of the city’s downtown area.
  • The country of Nicaragua is about the same size as the state of New York.

History

  • The earliest people are assumed to have been related to Mexico’s Aztec and Mayan people. The Nicarao was one of the largest groups of early natives.
  • When the colonial Spanish came to the country under de Cordoba in the 1500s, they named it after these Nicarao Indians plus all the vast amounts of water the country had (agua is Spanish for water).
  • The Ruins of Leon Viejo in Nicaragua is the oldest city in all of Central America. It is over 1500 years old and is still occupied today. It was founded by the early Spanish settlers.
  • Britain came to the Caribbean coast from Jamaica and some other islands in the early 1800s and settled on the eastern coast but gradually ceded control back to Nicaragua during the following decades.
  • After the successful Mexican Revolution from Spain  on September 15, 1821, Nicaragua then won its independence from Spain as well.
  • Did you know Nicaragua had an American president?  In 1856 William Walker from Tennessee seized control and declared himself to be Nicaragua’s President. He wanted to turn it into a slave state for the United States. The following year the Nicaraguan people kicked him out, aided by Cornelius Vanderbilt.
  • Managua replaced Leon as the capital in 1858 because Leon and Granada couldn’t decide which of their cities should be the country’s capital. Neutral Managua was chosen instead.
  • When the U. S. Marines occupied the country in the 1920s and 30s, August Cesar Sandino led their Nicaraguan resistance. Today he is one of their national heroes and the Sandinista political party is named for him.
  • Violent opposition to government corruption led to a civil war in the late 1970s and the Sandinista guerrillas rose to power. Their support of rebels in  El Salvador led the U. S. to sponsor contra guerrillas against the Sandinista through much of the 1980s.
  • Nicaragua elected Violeta Chamorro, the first democratic woman president of any democracy in the world in 1990. She was the first female president of any Central American country and served until 1997.
  • Former Sandinista President Daniel Ortega lost three consecutive elections before finally being elected President in 2006 and reelected four years later. He is Nicaragua’s current President and changed the system to be able to lead a third term and potentially a fourth one.
